History

It all began with an earthquake, a fire and a small group of women determined to make a difference.

In April 1906, a small group of women joined together to respond to the San Francisco earthquake and fire by organizing a relief unit. They called themselves The Assistance League, raising money through society balls and theatrical events. They were so successful that the following year, they decided to become a permanent organization to "be of assistance to everyone who needs it".

As America entered the war in Europe, a philanthropist named Anne Banning organized the Los Angeles Red Cross unit in 1917, serving as Director. They raised funds through the Red Cross Shop, forming the model for later shops across the country.

In 1919, seeing the incredible amount of need right here in Los Angeles, Banning and a group of 12 friends organized Assistance League to provide food and clothing for local families adversely impacted by World War I. The League's first community service, Good Samaritan, was established and became the prototype for today's Family Service Agency.

Committed to doing more, in 1920, Mrs. Banning borrowed $15,000 from local bankers to purchase a frame house in Hollywood, where Learning Center for Young Children stands today. This would be the League's headquarters until 1964 when it moved into the Anne Banning Community Center, designed by noted architect Paul R. Williams. In 1923, The League was officially granted nonprofit status by the State of California.

Today, the League continues to help those in critical need in Los Angeles and serve as an anchor of the community, making Los Angeles a better, kinder, more compassion city
